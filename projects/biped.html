PiB — Planar Bipedal Robot

A RABBIT-class, five-link, four-actuator boom-constrained bipedal robot built for my MSc dissertation. Custom mechanical design, full ROS2 software stack, ODrive/CAN torque actuation, and a dual classical/RL control pipeline targeting dynamic walking.

WHAT IS PiB

PiB is a five-link, four-actuator planar bipedal robot constrained to move in a vertical plane via a boom. Its architecture matches the canonical RABBIT platform from academic literature — a point-foot, underactuated biped with one passive degree of freedom per step. This is not a coincidence: RABBIT is the platform on which Hybrid Zero Dynamics (HZD) was developed and validated, and the decision to match it unlocks direct access to that control theory lineage.

The project spans mechanical design, electronics integration, simulation, and locomotion control, with the end goal of demonstrating dynamic circular walking on the boom and comparing HZD-based classical control against reinforcement learning under shared metrics.

MECHANICAL DESIGN

The leg geometry uses a five-link parallelogram mechanism — thigh, shank, and a mirrored set of passive links that form the closed kinematic chain. The hip and knee joints on each leg are independently actuated. All structural components are 3D printed, with motor mounts and joint interfaces designed to meet stiffness requirements under walking loads while minimising leg mass.

Motor placement at the knee is proximal (motor body near the hip) to reduce the leg's distal inertia and keep the mass distribution favourable for swing dynamics — a decision informed directly by the RABBIT literature.

ELECTRONICS & ACTUATION

Each of the four joints is driven by a T-Motor MN7005 KV115 brushless motor through an ODrive S1 motor controller. The hip joints use a 5:1 gear reduction; knee joints use 8.666:1. All four ODrives communicate over a single CAN bus at 1 Mbps, daisy-chained with a termination resistor on the last node only. The central compute is a Raspberry Pi running Ubuntu and ROS2 Jazzy.

Joint position sensing uses CUI AMT21 encoders operating in event-driven RS-485 mode, providing high-resolution absolute position feedback without polling overhead. Multi-node CAN communication controlling two ODrives simultaneously has been validated, including square trajectory execution on the physical right leg.

SOFTWARE STACK

The ROS2 workspace is structured across six packages: biped_description (URDF and robot model), biped_gazebo (simulation), biped_control (controllers), biped_hardware (ODrive interface), biped_kinematics (IK and inverse dynamics), and biped_gait (trajectory generation).

The URDF models the full five-link geometry including parallelogram mimic constraints, with each T-Motor modelled as a separate inertial link. Gazebo Harmonic provides the physics simulation environment, with ros2_control as the hardware abstraction layer — the same controller code runs in simulation and on hardware by swapping the interface plugin.

Pinocchio handles all dynamics mathematics: inverse kinematics, inverse dynamics for torque computation, and will serve as the dynamics model inside the trajectory optimiser for HZD.

CONTROL STRATEGY

Walking on a point-foot underactuated biped cannot be achieved with kinematic position control alone — impacts, swing dynamics, and ground reaction forces require torque-based control with a dynamics model. The control architecture targets a 500 Hz loop: read joint state from encoders, compute required torques via Pinocchio inverse dynamics, send over CAN to ODrives.

The classical control baseline uses Hybrid Zero Dynamics with virtual constraints parameterised by Bézier polynomials over a phase variable. Gaits are generated via trajectory optimisation (direct collocation), producing periodic orbits whose stability is verified through a scalar Poincaré return map. The RL pipeline runs in Isaac Lab with a PPO agent; the same Gazebo environment is used to evaluate both controllers fairly under shared metrics.

METRICS & COMPARISON

Both controllers will be evaluated on: forward walking speed, cost of transport (CoT = E/mgd), peak and RMS joint torque relative to ODrive limits, robustness under lateral perturbation, and development complexity. Using Gazebo as the shared evaluation environment ensures the comparison is not contaminated by physics engine differences.
