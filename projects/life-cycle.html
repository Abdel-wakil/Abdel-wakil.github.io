Life Cycle Management

Quantified life cycle analysis and topology-optimised redesign of a Roll-A-Car 740 mechanical lifting jack, reducing its carbon footprint from 560 to 440 kg CO₂, a 22% improvement, while preserving full structural performance.

MODULE
Life Cycle Management
PERIOD
Sep 2025 – Jan 2026
PRODUCT
Roll-A-Car 740 Jack
OUTCOME
22% CO₂ reduction

CONTEXT

The Roll-A-Car 740 is a standard mechanical lifting jack, a mature product with a well-established design. The project brief was to treat it as a case study in environmental redesign: quantify its carbon footprint through a full life cycle analysis, identify the dominant contributors, and then use simulation-driven topology optimisation to reduce them without compromising structural integrity.

BASELINE LCA

A full life cycle analysis established a baseline carbon footprint of approximately 560 kg CO₂ across the product's life stages. Material production and primary manufacturing processes emerged as the dominant contributors: the bulk of the embedded carbon is committed at the point of manufacture, not during use or end of life for a product of this type.

This finding focused the redesign effort clearly: reducing mass, and therefore material, was the highest-leverage intervention available.

TOPOLOGY OPTIMISATION

The redesign used topology optimisation in ANSYS, with the objective of minimising material volume subject to the constraint that structural performance (stress distribution and deflection under rated load) remained within the original design envelope. The optimisation was run with a mass retention target of 55–60% of the starting mass, establishing how much material could be removed while maintaining a meaningful safety margin.

The topology result identified which internal regions of the structural members carried negligible load and could be hollowed or removed. These regions were then interpreted into a manufacturable SolidWorks redesign: topology optimisation produces organic geometry that requires engineering judgement to translate into a producible part.

STRUCTURAL VALIDATION

Following the topology-informed redesign, a static structural analysis was re-run in ANSYS to confirm that the performance of the redesigned jack was maintained. The simulation verified that maximum stress and deflection under rated load remained within acceptable limits: the mass reduction had not introduced any new critical stress concentration regions or exceeded the original safety margins.

RESULT

The redesigned jack achieved a total carbon footprint of 440 kg CO₂, a reduction of approximately 22% from the 560 kg baseline. Because material production is the dominant life cycle stage for this product, the mass reduction from topology optimisation translated almost directly into the final CO₂ improvement.
