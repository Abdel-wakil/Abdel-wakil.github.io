3D-Printed Robotic Arm

Iteratively developed from a 3DOF concept to a full 6DOF system — evolving the drive technology from planetary gearboxes through hybrid cycloidal-belt transmissions, with a two-layer ROS + Python control architecture.

PERIOD
Oct 2024 – Aug 2025
TYPE
Personal project
DOF PROGRESSION
3 → 5 → 6 DOF
DRIVE
Cycloidal–belt hybrid

WHY BUILD IT

The robotic arm started as a way to build hands-on intuition for the full stack of robotic system development — mechanical design, drive selection, manufacturing, and software integration — without the constraints of an academic brief. The iterative progression from 3DOF to 6DOF was deliberate: each version was a working system that exposed real limitations before the next version addressed them.

MECHANICAL EVOLUTION

The first iteration established the basic architecture: a 3DOF shoulder-elbow-wrist chain using planetary gearboxes for torque multiplication at each joint. Planetary drives were chosen initially for their compactness and availability, but in practice the backlash was significant enough to affect end-effector positioning repeatability.

By the 5DOF version, the drive strategy had evolved toward hybrid cycloidal-belt transmissions — cycloidal stages for the high-torque shoulder and elbow joints, belt drives for the lighter wrist axes. Cycloidal drives offer dramatically lower backlash than planetaries at comparable reduction ratios, at the cost of more complex geometry to print reliably. Standard 608-2Z ball bearings were used throughout to keep hardware requirements minimal and replaceable.

The 6DOF final version refined the structural geometry, tightened the clearances at the cycloidal stages, and standardised on Nema 17 steppers across all joints — selected for their availability, cost, and compatibility with standard motor drivers.

CONTROL ARCHITECTURE

The control system is split into two layers with a clean interface between them. The high-level layer runs ROS and MoveIt inside a virtual machine: the operator defines target end-effector poses in RViz, and MoveIt's inverse kinematics solver computes the corresponding joint angles [q₁, q₂, q₃, q₄, q₅, q₆]. These joint angles are published as ROS messages.

The low-level layer is a Python script running on the physical hardware that subscribes to those joint angle messages and translates them into stepper motor step and direction commands in real time. This separation keeps the motion planning logic entirely in the ROS ecosystem while the hardware interface remains lightweight and responsive.

MANUFACTURING & DESIGN FOR PRINT

Every structural part was designed to be printable on a consumer-grade FDM printer without supports where possible, and with minimal post-processing. Wall thickness, layer orientation, and infill density were specified per-part based on the loading direction — the cycloidal disc geometry in particular required careful orientation to avoid delamination at the tooth roots.

Hardware was standardised deliberately: identical bearings throughout, a single bolt size per joint assembly, and no custom-length fasteners. This made iterating and repairing significantly faster — wrong clearance on a cycloidal stage meant reprinting one disc and reassembling in under an hour.

WHAT THIS BUILT TOWARD

The arm project was the direct precursor to the bipedal robot dissertation. It established the working pattern of iterating physical hardware against software requirements, developed familiarity with ROS and motor control, and produced the 3DOF dynamics analysis that became the Robotic Control Design module project. The kinematic and control intuitions developed here transferred directly into the biped's architecture decisions.
